Hello, I am Emre Bilge

Engineer by heart, and a lifelong learner.

My Background

My first exposure to engineering was when I was 10 years old and disassembled an old iPod. Seeing electronic components behind such an interactive device struck me with an interest in hardware and how simple 1s and 0s make up our digital world. Ever since then, I have consumed everything tutorial and lecture I could find on the internet regarding how computers work. Currently, I am a Junior at Vanderbilt University studying Electrical Engineering and Computer Science. I am interested not only in the hardware that modern devices operate on, but also the interactive software systems that we use every day.

About my professional journey

My journey in engineering started out during a summer experience with Engineers without Borders. I worked on a project involving solar power with oxygen concentrators. It was my first professional exposure to engineering, the summer before I entered University. I was exposed to new concepts in biomedical engineering, particularly how to design and keep an emphasis on the client's perspective. I worked with an international team on this project during the height of the COVID-19 pandemic, and it was designed for low resource communities to be able to obtain oxygen without a direct connection to power. It was a turning point for me to see how one's work had a direct impact on improving someone's life. At the end of the project, we presented our prototype to the United States Department of State. Right after, I entered college at the University of Pittsburgh, where I initially studied industrial engineering. I chose to study there because it was one of the few schools in the country which offerred that major. I realized I was more interested in technical projects rather than overall systems when I took Physics 2: Electricity and Magnetism. The sheer difficulty of the course really pushed me to be a problem-solver and analytical thinker. It was at that moment where I switched majors to Electrical Engineering. That same semester, I took my first ever programming course. It was taught in MATLAB and C. We covered basic syntax, program structure, and used programming as a tool for engineering. This was interesting to me, and it solidifed my interest in Electrical Engineering. The summer after, I worked with a healthcare company to apply some of my software knowledge to improve the patient portal. I was able to learn a lot about software quality control, user experience, and a lot about backend engineering. At the end of my internship, I was proud to see how much the patient portal had improved, going from a list of healthcare providers to a real-time interactive map of available healthcare practitioners. I also worked on a feature to schedule appointments and make payments by integrating an API with the database. In the fall, I transferred to Vanderbilt University. There, they had a new major which combined my interests called Electrical & Computer Engineering or ECE. I took a circuits course and a programming course. At that time, my mind was entirely split between hardware and software, as I enjoyed both aspects. Unable to decide which path I want to follow, I ended up picking a double major with Computer Science. It was the best decision I had ever made, as I am now knowledgable in many different aspects involving computers. My predominant interests involve developing applications, designing algorithms, electronics, modeling and predicting data patterns. I am enjoying my studies at Vanderbilt eager to learn more about how computers work. I enjoy working with my peers who are some of the most brilliant people I know
